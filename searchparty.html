Project Overview

Search Party is a mobile first designed Web App that allows users to complete a sightseeing tour of their city, giving them riddles and challenges to complete that take them to interesting spots and reveals a unique fact once they arrive at the location.

Search Party was created as part of the UX Design intensive course program at CareerFoundry.

Duration: Six months.

My Roles: UX Researcher, UX Designer, UI Designer.

Tools: Adobe XD, Photoshop, Optimal Workshop, Usability Hub.

Design Approach

I utilised the Design Thinking Process as it allowed me to better understand and define the problem space. This was essential in order to discover and design an innovative solution that fulfilled users’ needs.

Design Thinking Process

01 - Empathize

I developed a list of current problems faced and created a problem statement for my users. The problem statement allowed me to determine, and stay focused on, exactly what needs required solving.

Possible Problems

Londoners want a social way to take gaming outside and enjoy playing with friends.

Building scavenger hunts are time consuming and difficult to tailor to your location.

Current scavenger hunt apps are costly and focused on finding physical items, rather than location based, meaning Londoners find it hard to sightsee for free.

Problem Statement

Our users need a way to quickly join tailored location focused scavenger hunts because they want to experience the must-see sights of their city, whilst still enjoying the company of their friends and having a fun day out.

We will know this to be true when we see that users are able to partake in local scavenger hunts with their friends and tailor the hunt stops and locations to their needs.

Competitor Analysis

Two main competitors were analysed in order to:

1. Identify under-severed opportunities in the market

2. Spot weaknesses in competitors’ user experiences and overcome them

3. Make better-informed decisions about my overall design strategy

Design Thinking Process

02 - Observe & Define

User Interviews

To further my understanding of the users I was designing for, I conducted three user interviews. These participants, aged between 21 and 30, represented my key target user demographic.

Goals

  1. To understand how users currently explored their local city.

  2. To document the pain-points users experienced when exploring their local city.

  3. To understand users’ experiences/views regarding scavenger hunt apps.

Participants

Affinity Mapping

I utilised affinity mapping as an effective way to group and understand the information collected during the individual user interviews.

Key Insights

Users welcomed the idea very strongly and loved the concept. Generally, users do not know much about their local city and are always looking for interesting ways to spend more time outdoors.
Marketing the app as a social competitive scavenger hunt and fun day out, answering easy fun riddles, rather than historical learning expedition, would be better received from my users.

User Personas

In order to put a human face to my research and narrative, and to ensure design decisions were always focused on the users, I created three User Personas, representing the majority of my target audience.

Design Thinking Process

03 - Ideate

User Journeys

I created user journey maps to aid me in visualising the process users would follow to accomplish a specific goal within the app.

User Flows

Through task analysis, I created user flows to keep the designs more user-centred.

Rather than focusing on individual pages, I was able to focus on the absolutely essential screens required for my users to achieve their goals.

Information Architecture & Site Maps

I created an initial site map to aid in the visualisation of the page hierarchy.

I then conducted open card sorting on eight participants with 26 cards to refine the site map.

Design Thinking Process

05-Test

Usability Test

Before moving on to the high-fidelity prototyping, I first conducted usability testing to ensure the core elements and functionality met users’ needs. Six moderated in-person tests held at participants’ homes. Participants aged 18-35.

Goals

The overarching goal was to assess the usability of the app, focusing on how easily users were able to complete tasks and navigate within the prototype.

Methodology

Test included a short briefing and task performance on the Search Party prototype - a combination of direct and scenario tasks were used.

Test objectivies

  1. Determine how participants feel about the onboarding and registration process.

  2. Observe how users navigate and organise a hunt in the app

  3. Determine whether users are able to understand game play.

Affinity Mapping and Rainbow Spreadsheet

I used affinity mapping to organise the results of the usability test - grouping comments into observations, positive quotes, negative quotes and errors.

I then used a rainbow spreadsheet to document severity and frequency of errors, coupled with possible solutions and the next steps I would take.

Key observations

Overall, the testing went well with all users able to complete all tasks,. However, Participants often had difficulty selecting a button, although the correct intention was always there.

The data gathered was used to iterate on my designs. Examples:

Problem vs Solution 1: Multiple attempts needed to click ‘OK’ button --> Larger font, button and touch area utilised.

Problem vs Solution 2: Difficulty finding the hunt icon even though tips are given. --> Now highlights the icon as the tips are given to communicate which icon is being mentioned.

Problem vs Solution 3: Home newsfeed icon unclear. 50% of users could not describe what the icon represented. --> Icon changed to depict a house which is universally recognised as homepage.

Problem vs Solution 4: Couldn’t figure out how to favourite the hunt. 50% of users didn’t understand the tick icon. --> Removed need for tick icon. Options shown earlier in hierarchy.

Preference test results

During the testing phase, I also conducted a preference test on two variations of the hunt browse screen. The test focused on what method users preferred for communicating the hunt difficulty level.

13 participants took part in the preference test with 85% preferring option B. Main reasons centred on option B looking more fun, vibrant and like a party compared to Option A.

Some comments were raised around the busyness of option B, but given the purpose of the app (fun, vibrant, social game) I believe that if users were provided with more context, and the opportunity to test the whole prototype, this comment would not have been raised.
